## Core Idea

**How to transcend class through capital investment?**

**Use the losses you can afford to bear, to pursue a return you 'cannot afford' to miss.**

This is not about stable profits, nor is it about going all-in for sudden wealth. It is the optimal solution for the risk-reward ratio.

Three mainstream views exist in the market, but none of them solve the problem of individual investors transcending class:

1.  **The 'Individuals are Doomed to Fail' Theory** - Believing one is destined to be exploited, thus avoiding the market.
2.  **The 'All-in for Sudden Wealth' Theory** - Wanting to win or lose everything in one go, leading to uncontrolled risk.
3.  **The 'Steady Development' Theory** - Wanting to get rich slowly, but lacking sufficient time.

This article proposes a fourth approach: **Use the minimal acceptable risk to pursue potentially massive, victory-defining returns.**

## The Problem Statement

As individual investors, the purpose of surviving and developing in the market is to achieve sustained wealth growth, ultimately aiming to transcend class.

We often yearn to discover a method for stable profits leading to success, or fantasize about seizing an opportunity for overnight riches. Both are often difficult to achieve: the former because market competition is fierce and strategies can never remain perpetually victorious; the latter because the risks are too high and often require heavy bets. There are many voices, such as the "individuals are doomed to fail" theory that claims one can never beat the market and investing is destined to lose money, the "all-in for sudden wealth" theory of betting it all, and the "steady development" theory that wealth accumulation must rely on time. These are erroneous views that do not solve the initially stated problem. You can always see people getting rich overnight in the market, and many others going bankrupt, with some floating in the market their entire lives. Consequently, many people simplistically attribute the problem to either ability or luck.

This is essentially a problem with how the issue is defined. The market has never clearly defined what the standard for "success" is. If an investor only focuses on metrics like "annualized return," "maximum drawdown," "profit-loss ratio," or "Sharpe ratio," they have never defined what the endpoint of investing is. If a return target is met this year, what about next year? When does the battle end? When can victory be declared? Fighting a war where victory can never be defined is destined to be impossible to win.

I wish to propose a new perspective on the survival and development strategy for individual investors in the market. **I believe that waging a protracted war is the only path for individual investors to transcend class.** Briefly, a protracted war refers to a strategic combination of **controllable losses, cumulative advantage, and aggressive pursuit of victory.** It is not a protracted war in the temporal sense, but rather a strategy to achieve leapfrog wealth growth through a publicly definable approach.

1.  The Principle of No Unnecessary Risk: Control the maximum rate of loss to ensure survival capability during weakness.
2.  The Principle of No Waste: Trade time for advantage. Use systematic trading to avoid emotional decisions and achieve continuous expansion of advantage.
3.  The Principle of No Delay: Utilize pyramiding (adding to winning positions) to aggressively pursue victory without procrastination, achieving leapfrog wealth growth.
4.  The Principle of No Ambiguity: Clearly define strategic objectives and victory conditions before committing, avoiding vague strategic disorientation.

I firmly oppose any strategic outcome that "permanently traps individuals in the market." This is an immense waste of human life and societal resources.

### **Refuting the 'Individuals are Doomed to Fail' Theory (Cynicism)**

Many believe that individual investors can never beat the market and are destined to lose money.

Their reasons roughly fall into 4 points:

1.  Individuals' limited information is at a disadvantage compared to the market's complete information.
2.  Individuals' limited capital is at a disadvantage compared to the market's infinite capital.
3.  Individuals' limited knowledge is at a disadvantage compared to professional teams or institutions.
4.  The market contains fixed "drainage" mechanisms, such as transaction fees, taxes, etc., which erode profit margins.

This view overlooks the learning capacity and adaptability of individual investors. The market is a game-theoretic system; the relationship between exploiters and the exploited is dynamic and symbiotic. If the exploited disappear, the exploiters lose their basis for existence. Moreover, this ecology forms a cycle, creating a dynamic equilibrium of triangular game relationships. Often, choosing an ecological niche is key to an individual investor's success, not whether one can obtain absolute advantage. Holders of the "doomed to fail" theory view the market too one-sidedly, ignoring its complexity and dynamism. **This is a typical mindset of seeing a flaw and negating the entire thing.**

Holders of this view often also believe that the ultimate winners are always large institutions and professional teams. However, professional teams also started as individual investors. They grew into professional teams through continuous learning and adaptation to the market. Institutions and individuals differ merely in form, scale, resources, and stage of development, not in essence. Individual investors can absolutely, through a protracted war, gradually accumulate experience and capital, ultimately achieving the goal of transcending class.

There is also a subtle variation of this view, which posits that a winner-takes-all scenario might eventually emerge, where individual investors can never survive in the market and will be entirely eliminated. If this were truly the case, the exploiters would lose their basis for existence, and the market ecology could not be sustained. Therefore, this view is also untenable.
