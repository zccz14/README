From the perspective of Eastern philosophy, this position subtly resonates with Laozi's "reversal is the movement of the Dao." CZ's "embracing finitude" is not passive compromise but wisdom of advancing by retreating—by accepting limitations, one can gain greater freedom.

CZ's discussion shatters this fantasy: the key issue is not creating more powerful monolithic intelligence, but designing systems capable of integrating finite intelligences.

### II. The Search for Meaning: From Nihilism to Reconstruction

#### The Problem Posed

In [README.md](../README.md), CZ writes:

> "If I hear the Way in the morning, I can die content in the evening. Although I don't want to die yet and am still pursuing success in the secular sense, this is also to gain the ability to experience more and thus understand more. Life is all about experience; it inherently has no meaning. Understanding everything is my meaning."

This passage contains a complete scheme for constructing meaning, touching on the core concerns of existentialist philosophy.

#### The Position in the Text

CZ's position can be broken down into the following levels:

1.  **Negation of Meaning**: Directly denies that life has a priori, essential purpose.
2.  **Reconstruction of Meaning**: After negation, it is not nihilism but active construction—"understanding everything is my meaning."
3.  **Instrumentalization of Meaning**: Secular success is a means; "gaining the ability to experience more and thus understand more" is the end.
4.  **Openness of Meaning**: This is a dynamic process; understanding is a never-ending pursuit.

> "If one day I am gone, these will be the raw materials for AI to resurrect me."

This sentence reveals the deep structure of CZ's meaning-construction scheme: knowledge, thinking, and writing are not only for present understanding but also for some future possibility.

#### Philosophical Parallels

This position engages in a profound dialogue with Sartre's "existence precedes essence." Sartre believed that humans first exist and then define themselves through their own choices. CZ's "it inherently has no meaning" echoes this view but goes further to propose a constructive scheme: endowing life with meaning through "understanding."

Compared to Camus's "absurdism," CZ's position both acknowledges the absurd (life inherently has no meaning) and transcends it (constructing meaning through understanding). In *The Myth of Sisyphus*, Camus proposed two ways to deal with the absurd: suicide or revolt. CZ chooses the latter, but the way he endows meaning is not Camus's "revolt" but "understanding."

Compared to Buddhist philosophy, CZ's "understanding everything" subtly resonates with Buddhism's "prajñāpāramitā"—reaching the other shore through wisdom. However, Buddhist "understanding" ultimately leads to transcendence and liberation from the secular world, while CZ's "understanding" still points to secular experience and exploration.

#### Deeper Interpretation

CZ's meaning-construction scheme reflects a modern predicament: when traditional theological meaning systems collapse, individuals must create meaning for themselves. However, CZ does not fall into purely subjective relativism—he chooses "understanding" as the source of meaning, a choice that has its own internal logic: understanding is cumulative, shareable, and constantly evolving.

Looking deeper, CZ's meaning-construction scheme embodies a **cognitivist view of life**: cognition is not only a means of acquiring knowledge but also the essential mode of existence.

Furthermore, CZ introduces the **Fractal Recursive Structure of Intent Alignment**:

#### Philosophical Parallels

From an epistemological perspective, CZ's model forms an interesting parallel with Polanyi's theory of "tacit knowledge." Polanyi distinguished between "explicit knowledge" and "tacit knowledge," considering the latter to be incompletely articulable and context-dependent. CZ's concept of "intent alignment" implies this distinction—some parts of human intent are explicitly expressible, while others are tacit and difficult to formalize.

From an ethical perspective, CZ's model echoes Kant's "categorical imperative"—the moral value of an action depends on whether it is done from duty, whether it can be universalized, and whether it treats humanity as an end in itself and not merely as a means.

From a political philosophy perspective, CZ's model structurally resembles Hobbes's "social contract theory." CZ's "Risk Control Triangle" can be understood as a kind of "technological contract"—establishing trust between humans and AI through systematic safeguard mechanisms.

#### Deeper Interpretation

CZ's theory of controllable trust touches on a deeper philosophical question: **What is the ontological basis of trust in a world of uncertainty?**

Traditional theories of trust often presuppose that the object of trust possesses some stable, predictable properties. But in a complex system, no system is completely predictable. CZ's model offers a new approach: not seeking the foundation of trust in certainty, but constructing trust within **controllable uncertainty**.
