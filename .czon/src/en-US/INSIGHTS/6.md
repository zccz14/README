### Mathematical Interpretation

This theory has a mathematical interpretation:

First, set the victory condition as wealth reaching a certain value $A$. The goal of all strategy is to achieve this victory condition. This victory condition differs for each person, depending on individual living needs and wealth goals.

Second, assume there exists a strategy $S$, which is a black-box investment machine. It inputs capital $x$ at time $t_0$ and outputs capital $y$ at time $t_1$. We can define this strategy function with 3 parameters. Once these 3 parameters are determined, the output capital $y$ is determined:

$$
y = S(x, t_0, t_1)
$$

This function is not a mathematical function with an analytical expression, but an abstract representation of a black-box investment machine. Its inputs are a time period and capital; its output is capital. Moreover, $x$ in $S$ also has an objective domain $[L, U]$, i.e., $L \le x \le U$. This represents the lower and upper limits of investment capital. Individual investors cannot invest less than $L$ or more than $U$. $L$ represents the market's minimum transaction amount; $U$ represents the market's capital capacity for this strategy.

Clearly, this defines a basic investment concept: between time $t_0$ and $t_1$, investing capital $x$ yields capital $y$. If $y > x$, the strategy is profitable; if $y < x$, it is loss-making.

Now, define our capital curve $F(t)$, representing the total profit/loss at time $t$. Our goal is for the capital curve $F(t)$ to reach the victory condition $A$ at some time $T_W$.

$$
\exists_{T_W}  F(T_W) \ge A
$$

Now, at our initial investment time $T_0$, we first need to define a bottom-line risk control line:

$$F(t) \ge - C \cdot (t - T_0)$$

Here, $C$ is a constant representing the maximum loss speed we canæ‰¿å—. The meaning of this risk control line is that we must ensure that at any time $t$, our capital curve $F(t)$ does not fall below this line. This ensures our survival capability in a weak position. The setting of $C$ varies from person to person, depending on individual cash flow capability. For example, investing 3000 yuan per month means $C = 100$ yuan/day.

An investment action can be defined as using strategy $S$ to invest capital $x$ ($0 < x < A$) during a time period $[t_0, t_1]$, thereby obtaining capital $y$. This action can be expressed as:

$$
F(t_1) = F(t_0) - x + S(x, t_0, t_1)
$$

Our goal is to, through a series of investment actions, ultimately make the capital curve $F(t)$ reach the victory condition $A$ at some time $T_W$, while ensuring that at any time $t$, the capital curve $F(t)$ does not fall below the risk control line.

Furthermore, we can define profit $P(t)$ as the floating profit above the risk control line:

$$
P(t) = F(t) - C \cdot (t - T_0)
$$

You will find that even without any investment action, $P(t)$ grows linearly over time. This is because the risk control line itself declines linearly, while the capital curve $F(t)$ remains unchanged without investment actions. Therefore, $P(t)$ grows linearly over time.

When $P(t)$ reaches $L$, we can use all of $P(t)$ as the investment capital $x = P(t)$ for the next investment action. This is the so-called **profit-based position sizing (pyramiding) strategy**.

$$
F(t_1) = F(t_0) - P(t_0) + S(P(t_0), t_0, t_1)
$$

This strategy, under continuous profitable conditions, can achieve rapid growth of the capital curve $F(t)$, thereby achieving leapfrog wealth growth. Because our purpose is to make $F(T_W) \ge A$, the only other outcome is $F(t) = -C \cdot t$, which means the capital curve $F(t)$ directly declines along the risk control line, waiting for the next opportunity.
